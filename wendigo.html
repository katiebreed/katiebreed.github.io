Dead of Night

I was making dinner for my kids when it came. Our dog, Velvet, started barking at the front door and I asked my eldest, Maria, to see why. Maria had left the room, and shortly after, there was a loud crash and Velvet stopped barking abruptly, accompanied by a shriek from Maria.

“Mom! Mom!” She came sprinting back into the kitchen, blood splattered on her purple nightgown, and a horrified look on her face.

“What happened?” I asked. I dropped the wooden spoon and grabbed the hand gun out of the drawer. I loaded the bullets in, cocked it then pointed it at the ground. I positioned myself in front of Maria and her younger brother, Oliver. Maria was hugging Oliver and crying, he was confused but frightened because of the blood. I heard nothing but the sizzling of the chicken on the stove.

“Maria, I need to know what happened.” I whispered, keeping my eyes on the doorway, hoping whoever was in the other room wouldn’t hear me.

Maria fumbled through her explanation. “I went to- You asked me to see what Velvet was barking at and she was barking and- and then the door- I don’t know, the door just.. blew into the room and it almost hit me. It was so close to hitting me but it hit Velvet instead and she went across the room so, so fast and then I looked out the doorway and there was something there, I don’t know what it was but it was big, it was so big. I..” She stopped speaking, but continued a few moments later. “I don’t- I don’t know what that is.” I was concerned.

She didn’t know what it was? A bear maybe? Why would it blow down the door? Could a grizzly do something like that? First I had to get the kids to safety. I turned to them, Oliver looked like a shell of fear and Maria sobbed as she clutched him.

“You two, go out back and hide in your tree house. Call the police, tell them someone armed and dangerous broke into the house.” I handed Oliver my phone because Maria was shaking. He nodded. I had given him a mission and he was determined to complete it at all costs.

“It’s not a person, Mom! That thing can’t possibly be human!” She was whispering, still conscious that there was something, person or animal, in the other room.

“Hush!” I glared. “Go outside. I won’t ask again. I will take care of this. I’ll be fine.” Oliver started dragging her by the arm in the other direction, towards the back door and Maria was crying even harder now. She halted in the doorway, pleading for me not to go, and I softly told her, “I promise,” and she went reluctantly. All I could think about was that the kids would be safe. The tree house was sealed and had a space heater in it. I turned my focus back on the door way and I heard the back door softly open and close. Now that it was silent, I could hear the faint sound of something crunching and ripping.

I mumbled to myself, “What the hell?” Then it dawned on me. Velvet. She was still there and was hit by the door. Whatever person or animal was in there was eating our dog. Thank god the kids didn’t hear it, because my stomach was churning. I swore under my breath and creeped towards the doorway with my gun raised, my footsteps silent, the sound padded by the rubber soles of my boots. I turned off the stove, not breaking eye contact with the inky black of the hall. I paused and let my eyes adjust to the darkness, still listening to the sickening crunch as it chewed on the bones of our pet. I felt sick.

My eyes adjusted fairly quickly, just enough to make out a silhouette, basked in the moonlight that streamed in from the missing door and windows. It wasn’t a full moon, but it was bright enough to see.

Maria was right. This wasn’t human, and it wasn’t a bear either. It was hunched over Velvet’s body, though its shadow didn’t allow me to see her. Its skeleton-thin frame was covered in fur and had huge hind legs, its long, thin tail swished against the floor before it curled around its hooves. Its arms were unnaturally long, brandishing claws roughly the size of my hand. It sported massive antlers that hit the chain of the ceiling fan that hung on my 10 ft ceilings, and when it ripped a chunk of meat off and slung its head back to gulp it down, the antlers hit the fan blades. Its face wasn’t just gaunt, it was a skull. It looked deer-like but there was no skin, no meat, no fur, to cover the bare bone. Is this thing the reason for the missing hikers and kids the locals warned me about?

I gasped and its head whipped around to face me, the eye sockets empty and black. How could it see me? Did it see at all, or just hear? It seemed to consider its options, and I stayed as silent as I could, but it slowly got up off of its haunches, keeping its head turned towards me. The moonlight glinted off of the blood that dripped from its jaw, a long tongue flicked out and licked the blood off of its muzzle. I won’t let it get to my kids. I aimed my gun at its head, willing my hands to stop shaking. It started coming towards me, the antlers scraping the ceiling creating an ominous sound. I aimed, and pulled the trigger.

Five. I missed. I immediately yanked the hammer back and pulled the trigger again.

Four. I missed again. The creature was startled by the noise, but by the second shot it had recovered and was lunging at me. I ducked behind the wall and cocked the gun again while running back into the kitchen. It came barreling around the corner into the hallway, and I aimed at its chest this time. I pulled the trigger.

Three. It hit left of center and it went straight through its chest. The thing staggered back and fell to its knees but was very much still alive. I ran the other way and barreled through the kitchen and into the living room. Behind me, I heard its hooves pound against the tile and shatter some.

“Oh my god oh my god.” I kept running. Adrenaline shot through my limbs and I rounded back around into the foyer. I shot a look over my shoulder, which was a mistake because I saw that looming figure tearing after me. Its form backlit by the living room lamps and I couldn’t make out any of its features. I ran past the gory mess of flesh and bones, nothing was left of our sweet puppy. I ran out of the open doorway onto our front patio. As I ran by, I grabbed a long stick that was leaning against the doorway. I ran through the door of the patio but quickly jumped to the side and leaned against the wall to the left of the door. It came out of the house behind me so fast it burst through the metal framing of the screened in porch, and into the snowy yard.

I needed to get this thing away from the kids. It was standing eerily still, listening for any sign of me. I held my breath and then the creature roared. It was deeper than the sound of a bear and it was much, much louder. So loud the trees near it shook and snow pummeled to the ground. I waited as it listened for me. I threw the stick as hard as I could and it landed past the creature. As soon as it hit the ground, the creature lunged for it and I ran in the opposite direction.

I had just entered the forest, making sure to keep a line of trees between me and the beast. As it barreled towards me, it knocked down the trees in its way.

“Oh Jesus Christ. I can’t outrun it!” I pulled the hammer back on my gun, spun around while running backwards, and aimed at its head and shot.

Two. It hit its shoulder instead. It staggered a bit and slowed but it was still gaining distance on me, so I took the opportunity and aimed at its thigh. I slowed down so that I could aim properly, and I took the shot.

One. It had gained on me, but the bullet hit right where I wanted it to. The beast stumbled forward, but during its fall it slashed my left arm with its massive claws and I fell with it. The beast and I smashed into the ground at the same time. I fell on my butt and tried to scramble away from the thing, but it reached out and grabbed my calf, its claws ripped my flesh as I struggled out of its grasp and I shrieked. I quickly got up and started running again. Adrenaline rushed through my veins.

The kids. I needed to keep them safe.

It took its time getting up. I wiped at my face as I ran. The forest was dark, with the occasional moonlight filtering in, and the trees resembled looming demons. My body ached and the cold air cut into my lungs. I held my injured arm to my stomach which caused my side to cramp, and my calf was burning regardless of the chill. I could feel my blood freezing my pants to my leg around the gash in my calf. I weaved between trees and raced across an iced over river the best I could. It tried to cross the river but its hooves gave it even less traction than I had. I hid behind a tree on the other side to catch my breath and I heard the beast using its claws to drag itself across the ice, and before I knew it, it was on my side and it was silent.

The branches swayed in the wind and it listened for me. I heard it walk away and I felt immense relief. I was safe at last. I sat down against the tree, and a deer came barreling past me. My heart started pounding again. It’s still here. It’s waiting for me. Has it already found me? I scanned the trees from where the deer came from and waited, my gun aimed out in front of me. I had one bullet left. I waited, and listened. I heard a thud to the left and aimed in that direction, but the beast launched itself from behind a tree to the right. I swung my gun to aim at its head and I waited until it got closer. I needed to hit it. Everything seemed to slow down. It was rushing at me but it seemed to move slowly. It was almost on me now. I aimed up, and shot. The bullet went straight through its skull and it toppled head first into my lap. It smelled tangy like blood but it also smelled rotten. Its empty sockets stared up at me and its body was cold and motionless. I sighed in relief. Then, I heard sirens in the distance.

“Thank god.” I sighed. “The kids will be ok. I need to get back to them, but-” My head sagged as I saw my injuries. “I won’t make it. Will they ever find me out here?”

My head started spinning from the loss of blood. I dragged my blood-soaked finger through the slush to write out ‘I love you Maria, Oliver.’ I let my arms drop and the gun fall out of my hand, empty. I looked back at my path from the house, my breath becoming uneven and shallow.

“They’re safe. They’re safe.” I repeat like a mantra, but I hear a sound in the far distance and my heart freezes. Too similar to the roar that creature made to be anything else. Nonetheless, darkness closes in on my vision, still looking to the house.
